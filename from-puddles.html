Glass, Duckweed, Water

‘Science depends crucially on its own ontologies, so very different from commonsense ontologies, painstakingly assembled from diverse shards of evidence as a mosaic is assembled from thousands of tiny stones of diverse color and shape. It is observation, grounded in trained, collective, cultivated habit, that fuses these bits and pieces into a picture… And it is the picture, seized at a glance, all at once, that guarantees the sturdy existence of a world.’ - Lorraine Daston, On Scientific Observation, 2008

These pieces are made utilizing a process of getting glass powder to float, not through changing the chemical composition of water or glass, but through asking other materials for help. Using the result, I work with the kiln to create these topographical forms that hold patterns that reflect algae on top of water - nature on scales both big and small. By using a reflective surface, water, and duckweed, I muddy the viewing narrative of the piece, resulting in confusion as to which part of the piece is made out of what material. This resulted in a unique experience: due to the surreal visual nature of the glass, it demands that we try and find a narrative to understand what it might be like. However, this is counterbalanced by the ambiguity of the material composition of the piece, people were constantly asking me what the materials were, and I received many different comments of what they had assumed: some thought that the duckweed was the colors on the glass, some thought the duckweed was glass,some thought the water was glass, and some even didn’t realize the reflection was a reflection at first. What resulted was something I felt captured the idea that I had been thinking about for a long time: the way we use stories to make supernatural and random into natural and predictable into manmade and controllable. Not only was I very proud of how the work actually looked, but it really had felt like I had ‘invented’ a process, and like I had formed a stronger relationship with my place in the world and the nature around me. In aiming to create a work that was referencing the anthropomorphization of nature, I ended up doing it myself, which I find very fitting
