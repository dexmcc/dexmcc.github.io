In 1.1 Million, I made a structure that holds a glass cloud under a water system that sprays mist that condenses onto the glass, moving along its curves, and eventually forms droplets that fall down onto the viewer as rain. With it, I aim to ask the question, ‘how big is the rain?’ It’s quite big and overwhelming, if you consider the space it takes up. However,when faced with the mighty rain jacket, it becomes small and weak, defeated by a simple garment. When we look up to the sky, we see clouds and think of them as fluffy objects that remind us of cotton. However, the average cumulus weighs around 1.1 million pounds, and many could easily fit our entire human race within them. We create images, fictions, and narratives of what is around us to understand both the things that exist in a more ethereal state like emotions and feelings, but also the physical world around us and its unimaginable scale. It’s virtually impossible to truly understand the size or presence of most things, but we use fictions to label certain traits as important, so we can ignore the rest, and feel confident that we understand them. We aspire to feel like we can grasp the ‘all-at-once-ness’ of them. The fact that we rely on these fictions can be something quite beautiful, and allows the physical world to bend to our imagination and become fanciful and playful: a wavy glass structure held under a set of misters becomes an indoor rain cloud. However, the same tool can also be used to hide pain and exploitation; an unbelievably large network of fiberglass cables underneath our feet, built on the backs of those less fortunate than us, becomes an invisible cloud that floats high above our heads, that will never rain on us.
